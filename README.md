# ResearchSynthAI

How to use AI to run better literature searches, build systematic search strategies, synthesize evidence, and map citations around a topic.

## Topic

This talk focuses on practical AI methods for evidence discovery and synthesis.
It covers the full workflow: finding high-quality papers, designing a reproducible search strategy, synthesizing findings, and building citation/literature maps.
The goal is to help researchers move faster while improving transparency and rigor.

## Objectives

- Find a small set of high-quality, highly relevant papers quickly.
- Build a defensible, reproducible (systematic) search strategy with AI support.
- Synthesize evidence in a structured way without losing nuance.
- Understand influence and gaps using citation/literature mapping.
- Compare the best AI-enabled tools for each stage.

## Workflow Overview

1. Define scope and inclusion/exclusion criteria.
2. Use AI to generate keywords, synonyms, and controlled vocabulary terms.
3. Build and refine Boolean search strings for multiple databases.
4. Screen and prioritize candidate papers with AI-assisted triage.
5. Extract evidence into a structured table.
6. Use AI to draft synthesis while verifying every claim against sources.
7. Build citation maps to identify core papers, clusters, and missing areas.

## 1) Finding a Few Good Papers with AI

- Start with a seed question and ask AI for keyword variants, narrower/broader terms, and related concepts.
- Retrieve initial papers from trusted indexes (for example: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex).
- Ask AI to rank papers by relevance, recency, study design quality, and citation signal.
- Validate top picks manually: abstract fit, methods quality, and whether conclusions match results.

## 2) AI-Assisted Systematic Search Strategy

- Prompt AI to propose:
	- Population/problem terms
	- Intervention/exposure terms
	- Comparison terms (if applicable)
	- Outcome terms
	- Synonyms and spelling variants
- Convert to database-specific syntax (for example: MeSH for PubMed, title/abstract fields, truncation).
- Keep a search log: query versions, date searched, database, and result counts.
- Pilot and iterate: check if known key papers are retrieved and adjust sensitivity/specificity.

## 3) Evidence Synthesis with AI

- Use a standardized extraction form (study type, sample, methods, outcomes, limitations).
- Ask AI to summarize each paper into consistent fields, then compare side by side.
- Generate thematic groupings or evidence tables.
- Use AI for first-draft narrative synthesis, but verify all claims and numbers against source text.
- Mark uncertainty explicitly: conflicting findings, risk of bias, heterogeneity.

## 4) Citation/Literature Mapping

- Build backward and forward citation sets from seed papers.
- Use AI to cluster papers by topic, method, or population.
- Identify:
	- Foundational papers (high centrality)
	- Emerging clusters (recent but fast-growing)
	- Gaps (weakly connected or under-studied areas)
- Turn the map into figures and a short interpretation for your talk.

## 5) Useful AI Tools for Search and Synthesis

- Discovery and citation graph tools: Semantic Scholar, Connected Papers, Research Rabbit, OpenAlex-based tools.
- Academic search platforms with AI features: Scopus AI, Web of Science Research Assistant, Elicit.
- Screening and systematic review support: ASReview, Rayyan (AI-assisted workflows where available).
- Writing and synthesis support: GPT-based assistants, Claude, Perplexity (with strict source verification).
- Reference management + notes: Zotero with AI plugins/workflows.

## Good Practice Checklist

- Treat AI as a copilot, not an authority.
- Verify every important claim in the original paper.
- Preserve reproducibility with saved queries and decision logs.
- Report limitations of AI assistance in your methods.
- Watch for hallucinations, citation errors, and bias.
